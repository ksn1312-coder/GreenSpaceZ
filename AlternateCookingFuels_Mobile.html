Executive Summary

India's kitchen is the frontline of its energy security battle. The time to diversify is now.

India stands at a critical energy crossroads. With over 330 million LPG connections and annual consumption exceeding 31 million tonnes — against domestic production of just 13 MT — the country imports more than 68% of its cooking fuel. The ongoing geopolitical disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz have brought this vulnerability into sharp relief, triggering supply shortages across Kerala, Gujarat, and Maharashtra in early 2026.

This white paper examines the landscape of alternate cooking fuels available to India, their economic viability, environmental credentials, and the critical role that IoT-enabled monitoring and ESG-aligned policy can play in accelerating the transition. The core thesis: India's clean cooking revolution must go beyond LPG to a diversified, technology-enabled, locally-resilient energy mix.

02

The Crisis: India's LPG Dependency

Imports have surged from approximately 6 MT in FY2012 to 21 MT by FY2025, driven by the success of PMUY which expanded clean cooking access to near-universal coverage. Over 103 million PMUY beneficiaries now depend on subsidised LPG.

  • Import dependence: Over 68% of LPG consumed is imported, primarily from Saudi Arabia and the Middle East.
  • Storage deficit: India has only two underground LPG storage caverns — combined capacity is just two days of national consumption.
  • Price volatility: When global propane prices exceed USD 700/MT, domestic retail prices cross ₹1,000 per cylinder, straining low-income households.
  • Subsidy burden: LPG prices in Delhi rose 45% from FY2019–20 to FY2024–25, requiring continuous government intervention.
  • Connection-consumption gap: Despite 115% household coverage, 40% of India's population still cooks primarily on solid fuels.

The 2026 supply disruption has also exposed the commercial kitchen sector's fragility. The Ministry of Environment temporarily permitted restaurants and hotels to switch to biomass pellets and coal — a step backward on emissions that underscores the urgency of building alternatives.

03

Alternate Cooking Fuels — Overview

Four credible, scalable alternatives to LPG exist today. Tap each card to explore.

⚡
Electric Cooking (E-Cooking)
▼

The strongest near-term alternative for urban India. IEEFA's 2026 analysis confirms electric cooking is now approximately 20% cheaper than LPG and 14% cheaper than PNG in cities like Delhi. With India's renewable energy share growing, e-cooking delivers lower cost and a shrinking carbon footprint. Induction cooktops are energy-efficient, safe, and require no import chain. New York State mandated all-electric buildings from 2026 — India can lead a similar urban transition.

Key Advantages
No import dependency — powered entirely by the domestic grid
~20% cheaper than LPG post the recent ₹60 price hike
Highest cooking efficiency of any method (85–90%)
Enables dual-use electricity connection — no separate fuel infrastructure
IoT-ready: smart meters and load monitoring can optimise energy usage
🌿
Biogas / Compressed Biogas (CBG)
▼

India's most indigenous and circular cooking fuel. The country has a 160-year history with biogas — the first anaerobic digestion plant was built in Bombay in 1857. Over 5.1 million family-type biogas plants are installed with a combined daily capacity of 4.43 million cubic metres. CBG can directly substitute LPG in both households and commercial kitchens. Gujarat's Mujkuva village and solar-steam kitchens near Vadodara are live proof this model scales.

Key Advantages
Zero import dependency — feedstock from kitchen waste, dung, and agri-residue
Circular economy model: biogas slurry is a rich organic fertiliser
Near-zero carbon emissions with proper plant management
IoT sensors can monitor gas yield, pressure, and safety in real time
Strong ESG narrative — ideal for corporate and rural CSR programmes
☀️
Solar Cooking
▼

A fuel-free future for one of the world's sunniest nations. IOCL's advanced solar cookers with integrated panels are priced at approximately USD 500 per unit with zero lifecycle fuel cost. While upfront cost remains a barrier, solar-steam community kitchens near Vadodara are already feeding thousands daily. Carbon credits and green financing mechanisms can rapidly bring costs to accessible levels.

Key Advantages
Zero ongoing fuel cost post installation
Carbon credits can offset the capital expenditure significantly
Ideal for schools, temples, community kitchens, and SME canteens
IoT integration: irradiance sensors can automate cooking schedules
Perfect ESG showcase asset for corporates and social enterprises
🔥
Improved Biomass Cookstoves (ICS)
▼

The critical bridge technology for the 40% of Indian households still on solid fuels. Improved cookstoves burn biomass 40–60% more efficiently than traditional chulhas, dramatically reducing indoor air pollution. They are the most accessible entry point in the energy transition for remote and tribal populations where LPG distribution infrastructure is absent.

Key Advantages
Immediate health impact — reduces indoor PM2.5 by up to 70%
No fuel cost for households with access to agricultural waste
Accessible bridge technology for remote and tribal populations
IoT-enabled cookstoves can track emissions for carbon credit certification

The IoT & ESG Opportunity

Smart kitchens are the next frontier. IoT + Clean Fuel = Measurable ESG outcomes.

The clean cooking transition is not just an energy challenge — it is a technology and data opportunity. IoT-enabled cooking infrastructure creates measurable, verifiable ESG outcomes that can be monetised through carbon markets, reported in sustainability disclosures, and tracked by regulators in real time.

🔬 Smart Biogas Monitoring
Sensors on biogas plants track gas yield, methane quality, temperature, and pressure — enabling predictive maintenance and remote diagnostics.
📊 E-Cooking Load Analytics
Smart plugs and meters on induction cooktops provide household-level consumption data to utilities for demand forecasting.
☀️ Solar Cooker Optimisation
Irradiance and temperature sensors automate cooking schedules and alert users to optimal cooking windows.
📋 Emissions Tracking
Cookstove IoT modules measure combustion efficiency and generate verifiable carbon credits under VERRA/Gold Standard frameworks.
🗺️ Supply Chain Intelligence
Real-time clean cooking dashboards for policymakers combining LPG tracking, biogas plant yield monitoring, and distribution analytics.

Policy Recommendations

India has demonstrated that policy can drive massive energy transitions — PMUY's success is proof. The same architecture must now be applied to the next wave of clean cooking fuels.

01
E-Cooking Incentive Scheme
Introduce targeted subsidies for induction cooktop purchase in urban households, mirroring PMUY's LPG model. A one-time subsidy of ₹2,000–3,000 per unit would accelerate adoption among middle and lower-income groups.
02
CBG Scale-Up Mission
Fast-track the National Bioenergy Programme — target 10 million biogas plants by 2030. Mandate biogas connections in new housing societies and require kitchen waste segregation at source.
03
Carbon Credit Certification for Clean Cookstoves
Develop an India-specific carbon credit standard for IoT-verified clean cookstove emissions reductions. This unlocks private capital and creates sustainable revenue streams for rural energy entrepreneurs.
04
Smart Kitchen Infrastructure Fund
Create a dedicated ₹500-crore fund for IoT-enabled cooking infrastructure in public institutions — schools, hospitals, defence canteens, and government offices.
05
ESG Disclosure Mandate for Energy Companies
Require oil marketing companies and cooking fuel distributors to disclose Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions, import dependency ratios, and clean fuel transition roadmaps in annual BRSR filings.
07

The Road Ahead

India's alternative fuels market is projected to grow at a CAGR of 13% from 2025 to 2030, reaching USD 40.4 million by 2030. Hydrogen — while nascent — is the fastest-growing segment for the next decade. The trajectory is clear: India's cooking energy mix will diversify, and those who build the infrastructure, technology, and financing mechanisms for this transition will define the next energy economy.

2026–27
Induction cooktop adoption reaches 50M urban households; Smart meter rollout covers 20M e-cooking connections
2027–28
10 million biogas plants operational; CBG blending mandate introduced in commercial kitchens
2028–29
Carbon credit market for clean cookstoves reaches ₹1,000 crore annually; IoT monitoring deployed in 1M+ biogas units
2029–30
LPG import dependency drops below 50%; E-cooking becomes the primary fuel in urban India
